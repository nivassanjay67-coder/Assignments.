A Bonfire with a Greater Purpose

Pile Burning for Forest Health and Resilience

The picture above looks like a guy enjoying a good bonfire, but really, that’s Mike Winter, one of our dedicated Stewards, assisting at a Pepperwood Pile Burning Workshop (check our schedule for the next one). During these workshops, participants learn how to safely and legally use pile burning as a strategic fuel reduction method while also taking an active role in the process. At Pepperwood, we implement sustainable forest thinning and pair it with strategies like pile burning to create defensible space and reduce excess woody materials or fuels.

Pile burning is often the most efficient method for managing flammable materials. An added benefit is that burning can be strategically placed to reduce invasive species or other undesired vegetation while creating a nutrient-rich scar on the ground that is an excellent opportunity for native plant restoration. Together, these land stewardship strategies help make our forests, grasslands, and chaparral healthier and ultimately more fire resilient.

The Cycles of Stewardship

Stewardship is a cyclical process, much like the changing seasons. The burn scars left after these pile burns create ideal conditions for new vegetation to grow. Often, the first plants to take advantage of these nutrient-rich spaces are opportunistic species. If we’re not careful, invasive plants can gain the upper hand. Instead, we prioritize hardy native grass species, whose root systems stabilize the soil and utilize water resources more effectively. Our stewardship team, along with volunteers, plants grass plugs—small, pre-rooted pieces of grass, soil, and roots grown in trays—to quickly re-establish these burn scars. Over the last five years, more than 120,000 native grass plugs have been planted across the preserve.

The efforts of our stewardship team, along with our dedicated group of volunteers and Stewards, play a crucial role in maintaining the health and resilience of our forests, grasslands, and chaparral. Through practices such as Douglas fir and brush encroachment removal and pile burning, we not only reduce the risk of wildfires, create defensible space, and increase the safety of ingress and egress, but also enhance the ecosystem by reducing invasive plants and promoting the growth of native vegetation.

We study the impacts of these treatments through our comprehensive ecological monitoring (for instance, the streamflow enhancement project) to ensure that each application is working towards the goal of making the ecosystem healthier. Together, we are fostering a more vibrant and sustainable environment, ensuring that our forests can thrive for generations to come.
